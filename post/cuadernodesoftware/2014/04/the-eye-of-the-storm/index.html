The eye of the storm

Escrito por Guille en la bitácora Cuaderno de software, artículo original
Publicado el viernes, 25 de abril de 2014 a las 20:28 UTC
Etiquetado como desarrollo-de-software

I’m afraid that this is one of those posts in which the author has to briefly summarize its recent history to make a point at the end. Keep on reading if you are interested in personal awakenings, coding and the sexiness of hexagonal structures…

TL;DR Everything is about the secret of steel.

At first I was alone, misunderstood, working for a company that wasn’t about software at all. I didn’t have a clear view of the path ahead of me, but thanks to Luis and David and other people I started my journey of continuous improvement, learning as much as I could about coding, design patterns, agile principles, testing, katas, integration, etc.

Then I started working at a more technology-friendly organisation, BuntPlanet. My first task was to harness a legacy Struts 1.2, Ibatis 1 using, Java 6 project – eeeeewww, you’d probably scream. But I embraced the opportunity, applying what I had learned so far.

At BuntPlanet, I started a small greenfield project for which I choose Grails 1.3 as technology stack. I soon realized that the weight of the framework wasn’t worth it. I began to think of ways to extricate myself from the framework, and DDD came into my life.

Another opportunity came along. I set up another greenfield project with a micro services approach and a frontend using AngularJS. I worked my way through the project very happily, and it really felt this framework wasn’t going to be a problem. And indeed at the end it seemed good framework.

During this time, my love for JavaScript had grown stronger than ever, but I was forced to leave the project for some months to do some consulting for another company where we built a walking skeleton with a very complete technology stack addressing security, persistence, templating, concurrency model… Again, lots of libraries and frameworks, and a great challenge to create a clean architecture out of the mess.

Currently I’m starting another new greenfield project with AngularJS in the frontend and Spark Java in the backend. I’m very proud to say that, Luis and I are implementing a very good looking, sexy hexagonal architecture from which we’re learning truly A LOT about programming and real TDD.

There has been some buzz these days about a blog post from David Heinemeier Hansson, creator of Ruby on Rails and CTO at Basecamp, among other things, claiming that TDD is dead. If you read the article and the opinions from people that agree with it, two main issues come up: TDD is seen as waste and most of the time it leads to an over-engineered design that makes the code harder to follow and maintain.

I can see myself there, a younger, dumber self a long time ago. Been there, done that. Reading the criticism, all the mistakes someone that doesn’t really understand what TDD is about emerge. Ian Cooper explains it way better than me in his video: TDD, where did it all go wrong? If you haven’t watched it, you really should. Right now. Watch it.

I also read many comments criticising AngularJS. They even adapt to AngularJS old funny sayings about factories and services that we used to apply to Spring Framework. I admit that some artefacts from the stack make no sense at all (services and factories, especially) but, hey, here I am TDDing my apps, applying the best design decisions I can make, producing an hexagonal-ish architectured projects that feel very right to me and others that work with me.

This demonstrations of frustration about techniques hard to master or frameworks that keep standing on the way look like a storm to me, and I feel like now I stand on the inner border, looking down into the eye of the storm where a land of steady practices and principles lays before me. I started to feel the storm and glance that eye 4 years ago, and I’ve been walking my journey to the centre ever since.

Doing TDD is easy. What’s hard is to write good code: to know where to apply the correct patterns, to master SOLID principles, to really and deeply understand hexagonal architecture… It’s also hard to communicate it to those who have the needs that you’re going to fulfil. This is key to define the behaviour that should drive your TDD flow.

And frameworks and libraries are only tools. I will choose the best that I can for the task ahead but they have neither importance nor inherent value by themselves. They don’t make the code good or bad. I do that when I use them.

In the end It’s all about the secret of steel.
